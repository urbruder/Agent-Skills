# Pricing Psychology — Presenting Price to Win

How you present price changes how it's perceived. Same number, different framing, different outcome.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Price comes after value, never before

Don't lead with price. Build the case for the outcome first. By the time the client sees the
number, they should already want to say yes. Price is the final confirmation, not the first question.

### 2. The number you say first sets the anchor

If you say "$8,000" and then offer "$6,500," $6,500 feels cheap.
If you say "$6,500" cold, it feels expensive.
Always anchor high if offering options.

### 3. Specificity signals precision, not expense

"$6,500" feels arbitrary. "$6,400" feels calculated.
"$10,000" feels like a round guess. "$9,750" feels like you did the math.
Specific numbers imply you know exactly what you're doing.

### 4. Never offer more than 3 options

More than 3 → decision fatigue → they don't decide → you lose.
The best number is usually 2.

## Option Structures

### Single Price (cleanest, most confident)
Use when: you know exactly what they need, there's one right answer.

```
[PROJECT NAME]: $[Amount]

• [X]% deposit to begin
• [X]% at [milestone]
• [X]% at delivery
```

Confidence signal: you're not asking them to choose. You've decided what they need.

---

### The Binary (most common for freelancers)
Use when: there's a natural "good" and "great" distinction.

```
[OPTION A NAME — outcome-focused]: $[Lower Amount]
[2-sentence description — what this covers, framed as complete]

[OPTION B NAME — outcome-focused]: $[Higher Amount]
[2-sentence description — what this adds, framed as the obvious upgrade]
```

**Naming matters:**
- Bad: "Basic" / "Premium"
- Bad: "Starter" / "Pro"
- Good: "Launch" / "Launch + Scale"
- Good: "Foundation" / "Full Build"
- Good: "Essentials" / "Complete"

**Pricing ratio:** Option B should be 40–70% more than Option A.
Too close → everyone picks A. Too far → B feels out of reach.

**Which do clients pick?** Middle option when there are 3. Higher option when there are 2 (if
framed correctly — Option B should feel like the "real" version, not an upsell).

---

### The Three-Tier (for agencies, larger projects)
Use when: you have a genuine good/better/best distinction.

```
[TIER 1 — floor]: $[X]
[What it includes — limited scope, specific constraint]

[TIER 2 — target]: $[Y]  ← design this as the "right" answer
[What it includes — the full thing]

[TIER 3 — ceiling]: $[Z]
[What it adds — speed, expanded scope, ongoing support]
```

**Make Tier 2 the obvious right choice.** Tier 1 should feel slightly uncomfortable (too limited).
Tier 3 should be clearly "more than most need."

## Framing Techniques

### The Monthly Reframe
For retainers or large projects, show the monthly equivalent:
> "$36,000 for the year — $3,000/month for a dedicated growth partner."

$3,000 feels smaller than $36,000, even though it's identical.

### The Comparison Frame
Anchor against something familiar in their world:
> "This is roughly the cost of one senior hire's salary for a month — without the equity, benefits,
> or ramp time."

Use their industry. Marketing clients: ad spend. Tech clients: SaaS tool costs. Agencies: one FTE.

### The ROI Frame
When you can calculate it, show the math:
> "If this increases your conversion rate from 1.5% to 2.5%, on your current traffic that's
> roughly $180K in additional annual revenue. The engagement is $18,000."

10:1 ROI framing. Make them do the math, then confirm it.
Only use this if the numbers are real and defensible.

### The Cost of Inaction Frame
Don't overuse, but sometimes right:
> "Every month this stays broken costs you [X]. This engagement resolves it in 8 weeks."

## Handling Price Objections

### "That's more than we expected."
→ "What were you expecting? I want to understand the gap."
(Learn the number. Don't drop your price before you know what you're negotiating against.)

### "Can you do it for less?"
→ "I can adjust scope. What would you take out?"
(Never cut price without cutting scope. Trading away margin for nothing trains clients to negotiate.)

### "We got a lower quote from someone else."
→ "That's worth understanding. What does their scope include that mine doesn't — or vice versa?"
(Reveal the comparison. Usually the scopes aren't equal.)

### "We don't have budget right now."
→ "Is there a version of this that fits your current budget, or is the issue timing?"
(Diagnose: no money vs. wrong quarter vs. not convinced of value yet. Each has a different answer.)

### "We need to run this by [someone]."
→ "Of course. What does [that person] typically need to see to feel comfortable moving forward?"
(Get ahead of the next objection. Offer to join the follow-up call.)

## Payment Structure Best Practices

**Standard three-part:**
- 33–50% deposit (to begin — non-refundable)
- 25–33% at midpoint milestone
- Remainder at delivery

**For longer projects (4+ months):**
- Monthly billing against a retainer
- Or milestone-based tied to deliverables

**Never:**
- 100% on delivery (you carry all the risk)
- Net 60+ terms (cash flow killer)
- "Invoice me when you're done" (no milestone = no leverage)

**Always include:**
- Due dates (not just "upon delivery")
- Late payment language (even if you never enforce it — it signals seriousness)
- What happens if project pauses or scope changes mid-flight
