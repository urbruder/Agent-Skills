# Winning Proposal

**The goal: get a yes before they read anyone else's.**

A winning proposal isn't a document — it's a closing argument. It proves you understand the
problem better than the client does, makes the decision feel obvious, and removes every reason
to wait or compare.

---

## Step 1: Extract Everything You Know

Before writing a word, pull every useful signal from what the user has shared:

**From a brief or job post:**
- What problem are they actually trying to solve? (Not the task — the business problem underneath)
- What outcome would make them celebrate? (Not "a new website" — "more leads from enterprise clients")
- What risk are they most afraid of? (Missing deadline, going over budget, bad quality)
- Any hints about past failures? ("We tried this before and it didn't work" = gold)

**From a discovery call description:**
- What did the client emphasize or repeat?
- What questions did they ask? (Questions reveal fears)
- Any budget signals, timeline pressure, stakeholder mentions?

**If the user gives you almost nothing**, ask ONE question:
> "What do you know about why they need this, and what's at stake if it doesn't go well?"

Don't ask for a brief rundown of the whole project. One question, then write.

---

## Step 2: Identify the Proposal Type

Choose based on the project size and relationship stage:

| Type | When to use | Length |
|---|---|---|
| **Flash Pitch** | Inbound lead, early stage, <$5K project | 1 page / 300 words |
| **Standard Proposal** | Post-discovery, most freelance/consulting projects | 2–4 pages |
| **Full SOW** | Larger retainers, agencies, enterprise clients | 4–10 pages + appendix |
| **Deck Pitch** | When presenting live, not emailing | See `references/deck-pitch.md` |

Default to **Standard Proposal** unless the user specifies otherwise or signals suggest another type.

## Step 3: Build the Proposal

### The Winning Structure (Standard Proposal)

Every section has a job. Do not skip sections or merge them.

---

#### Section 1: THE HOOK (The opening paragraph — do not call it "Introduction")

The client reads this and thinks: *"They get it."*

Write 2–4 sentences that:
1. Name the real problem (not the deliverable)
2. Acknowledge what's at stake
3. Signal that you have a specific answer (not "we'll figure it out together")

**Formula:**
> [Company/client name] is [specific situation]. The risk isn't [surface problem] — it's [real consequence]. This proposal outlines how we'll [specific outcome], without [specific fear they have].

**Example:**
> Meridian Financial has a website that explains what you do but doesn't convert. The risk isn't an outdated design — it's enterprise prospects landing on your site and quietly choosing a competitor who looks more credible. This proposal outlines how we'll rebuild your site to convert senior decision-makers, without a six-month redesign that stalls your Q3 push.

---

#### Section 2: WHAT WE HEARD (Understanding their situation)

The client reads this and thinks: *"They listened."*

3–5 bullet points. Each one should be something the client recognizes as true but hasn't seen written down clearly. These come from the discovery call or brief — paraphrase back their words, goals, and fears.

Format:
```
• You need [outcome] by [timeframe] because [business reason]
• Your current [thing] isn't working because [specific reason they gave]
• You're concerned about [risk] — especially [specific version of that risk]
• You've tried [past approach] and it [what happened]
• Success for you looks like [their definition, not yours]
```

If you don't have this intel, write the most likely version and note to the user:
> *(Flag: Confirm these assumptions match what you heard before sending)*

---

#### Section 3: THE APPROACH (How you'll solve it)

The client reads this and thinks: *"This is credible. They have a method."*

**Do NOT write a task list.** Task lists commoditize your work and invite line-item negotiation.

Write a phased narrative instead. 3–4 phases. Each phase:
- Has a name that sounds like progress (not "Phase 1")
- Describes an outcome, not activities
- Implies your specific expertise or method

**Phase naming examples:**
- Discovery & Diagnosis → Clarity & Direction → Build → Launch & Optimize
- Research → Architecture → Development → Handoff
- Audit → Strategy → Execution → Measurement

**Phase format:**
```
**[Phase Name]** — [Timeframe]
[2–3 sentences on what happens and why it matters. Focus on what they get, not what you do.]
```

---

#### Section 4: WHAT YOU'LL RECEIVE (Deliverables)

The client reads this and thinks: *"I know exactly what I'm buying."*

Simple bulleted list. Be specific. Don't over-promise or under-specify.

```
✓ [Specific deliverable, not a category]
✓ [Specific deliverable]
✓ [Number] rounds of revisions included
✓ [Final format/handoff detail]
```

**Optionally include what's NOT included** — this prevents scope creep and builds trust:
```
Not included in this scope:
× [Thing they might assume but isn't covered]
× [Common add-on that's separate]
```

---

#### Section 5: INVESTMENT (Pricing)

The client reads this and thinks: *"That's fair. I understand what I'm paying for."*

**Pricing presentation rules:**
- Never apologize for your price. State it plainly.
- Anchor with a name before the number: "The [Project Name] engagement: $X"
- If offering options, give exactly 2 or 3. Never 4+.
- Name the tiers by outcome, not by size: "Foundation" / "Full Build" — not "Basic" / "Premium"
- Always show payment structure: deposit + milestone + final is standard

**Single price format:**
```
**[Project Name]: $[Amount]**
• [X]% ($[Amount]) due at signing to begin work
• [X]% ($[Amount]) due at [milestone]
• [X]% ($[Amount]) due at delivery
```

**Two-option format:**
```
**[Outcome A]: $[Amount]**
[One sentence on what this covers]

**[Outcome B]: $[Amount]**
[One sentence on what this adds — make it feel like a natural upgrade, not an upsell]
```

---

#### Section 6: WHY US (Social proof / credibility)

The client reads this and thinks: *"They've done this before."*

Keep it tight. 1 relevant case study + 1 quote, or 3 quick proof points. Do NOT dump your whole portfolio here.

**Formula:**
> When [similar client type] needed [similar outcome], we [what you did]. The result: [specific measurable outcome]. [Optional: one-sentence quote from that client.]

If the user has no specific case study, write a placeholder:
> *(Insert: 1–2 sentence story about a past project most similar to this one. If you have a client quote, drop it here.)*

---

#### Section 7: THE CLOSE (Next steps)

The client reads this and thinks: *"I know exactly what to do next."*

Remove all friction. Be specific about the next action.

```
**Ready to move forward?**

Reply to this email and I'll send the contract. Or, if you'd like to talk through any questions
first, grab 20 minutes here: [calendly/booking link].

To hold your spot for a [Month] start, I'll need a signed agreement by [specific date].
[If true: I have one opening for this timeline. First to sign secures it.]

Looking forward to [specific thing about their project].

— [Name]
```

**Scarcity only if true.** Never manufacture urgency.

## Step 4: Tune for Context

After drafting, check these variables:

### Tone calibration
| Client type | Tone | What to emphasize |
|---|---|---|
| Startup / founder | Direct, energetic | Speed, ROI, scrappy resourcefulness |
| Enterprise / corporate | Professional, structured | Process, risk mitigation, track record |
| Creative / agency | Peer-level, stylish | Vision, craft, taste |
| Non-profit | Warm, mission-aware | Shared values, outcomes for people |
| Technical client | Precise, detailed | Methodology, specs, no fluff |

### Common mistakes to avoid
- **Talking about yourself too early.** The client's problem comes first — always.
- **Feature-listing instead of outcome-framing.** "We'll build 12 pages" vs. "You'll have a site that converts."
- **Price buried at the end.** Clients scroll for the number. Don't hide it.
- **Weak close.** "Let me know if you have questions" is not a close. Name the action.
- **No deadline.** A proposal with no expiry is a document, not an offer.

---

## Step 5: Flash Pitch (for small / fast / early-stage proposals)

When speed is the priority or project is small, skip the full structure.
Use this 5-paragraph format instead:

```
[Para 1 — THE PROBLEM THEY HAVE]
One sentence. Make them feel seen.

[Para 2 — YOUR ANSWER]
What you'll do and why your way is the right way.

[Para 3 — WHAT THEY GET]
3–5 bullets. Concrete deliverables.

[Para 4 — THE PRICE]
One number. Payment terms in one sentence.

[Para 5 — THE CLOSE]
One action. One deadline.
```

---

## Output Format

Always deliver:
1. **The proposal copy** — ready to paste into email or doc, no placeholders except flagged unknowns
2. **Flagged assumptions** — a short list of things to verify or personalize before sending
3. **Subject line** (if email delivery) — specific, not generic ("Your proposal" is not a subject line)

**Good subject lines:**
- `Proposal: [Project Name] for [Client Name] — [Outcome in 4 words]`
- `[Client Name] × [Your Name]: [Project] Proposal`
- `Here's how we'd approach [their goal] — [Your Name]`
