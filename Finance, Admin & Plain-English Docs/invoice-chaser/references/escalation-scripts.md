# Escalation Scripts & Language Reference

## When to Use This File

Read this file when handling Tier 3, 4, or 5 situations — invoices more than 3 weeks
overdue, clients who've been chased multiple times, or situations that may require
formal notices, late fees, work suspension, or handoff to collections/legal.

---

## Tier 3 — Direct Notice (22–45 days overdue)

### Key principles
- Name the consequence explicitly but without heat
- Give one specific deadline (not "soon" or "ASAP")
- State what happens after that deadline — and mean it
- Keep emotional language completely out

### Sample language blocks

**Opening (replaces "I hope this finds you well"):**
> "I'm writing regarding Invoice #[X] for [amount], now [N] days past its due date of [date]."

**Consequence sentence:**
> "If payment is not received by [specific date], I will need to apply the late fee outlined in our agreement and pause work on any active projects until the balance is cleared."

**CTA:**
> "Please reply to confirm you've received this, or let me know if there's an issue I can help resolve quickly."

---

## Tier 4 — Final Notice (45+ days, or 3rd+ ignored follow-up)

### Key principles
- This is not a request. It is a notification.
- Deadline is non-negotiable and imminent (5–7 days from send)
- Name the next step (collections, legal, credit reporting) only if you will actually do it
- No softeners, no apologies, no "I hope we can resolve this"

### Full template

**Subject:** Final Notice — Invoice #[X] — [Amount] — Due [Date]

> Dear [Name],
>
> This is a final notice regarding Invoice #[invoice number] for [dollar amount], which
> has been outstanding since [original due date] — now [N] days overdue.
>
> Payment in full must be received by [specific date, 5–7 days from now].
>
> If payment is not received by that date, I will [choose one that you will actually do]:
> — Refer this account to a collections agency
> — Pursue this matter through small claims court
> — Engage legal counsel to recover the outstanding amount plus applicable fees
>
> To make payment: [payment link / bank details / method]
>
> If you believe this notice has been sent in error, please contact me immediately at
> [email/phone].
>
> [Name]
> [Business name]
> [Contact]

## Tier 5 — Escalation Path (No Response After Final Notice)

At this stage, stop sending emails. Choose one of these paths:

### Path A — Small Claims Court
- Available in most jurisdictions for amounts under $5,000–$25,000 (varies by region)
- No lawyer required
- Filing fee: $30–$100 typically
- Success rate is high when you have a signed contract and paper trail
- **Before filing**: Send one certified letter (physical mail) as a final paper trail step

### Path B — Collections Agency
- Appropriate for amounts over $1,000 where relationship doesn't matter
- Agency takes 25–50% of collected amount as commission
- Damages the client relationship permanently
- Good agencies: Atradius Collections, Euler Hermes, national B2B collections services

### Path C — Demand Letter from Attorney
- Effective psychological trigger — many clients pay immediately upon receiving this
- Cost: $100–$500 for a simple letter
- Works well for amounts over $2,000
- Can be the step before small claims or full litigation

### Path D — Chargeback / Payment Platform Dispute
- Only applicable if you were paid via Stripe, PayPal, etc. and need to dispute a refund they've issued, or if a deposit was paid and work was completed
- File a dispute with the platform directly with contract + delivery evidence

### Documentation you need regardless of path:
- Signed contract or written agreement (email confirmation counts)
- Original invoice with due date
- All follow-up email threads with timestamps
- Any acknowledgment from the client (even "I'll pay next week" counts)
- Proof of delivery (work files, email with deliverables, project handoff)

## Stop-Work Notice Language

Use when: invoice is 30+ days overdue on an ongoing project, and you need to halt work
without damaging the relationship more than necessary.

**Subject:** Project Work Paused — Invoice #[X] Outstanding

> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm writing to let you know that I'll need to pause work on [project name] until
> Invoice #[X] for [amount] (due [date]) is settled.
>
> This isn't something I take lightly — I want to keep the project moving as much as
> you do. As soon as payment clears, I can resume immediately and get [next milestone]
> completed by [estimated date].
>
> To pay now: [payment link]
>
> Please let me know if there's anything on your end causing a delay — happy to discuss.
>
> [Name]

**When to use Stop-Work**: Only when you have leverage (ongoing work, upcoming deliverables).
Do not use if the project is already complete — you've lost your leverage and this just
antagonizes the client.

## Late Fee Application Language

If your contract includes a late fee clause and you're now applying it:

> "Per our agreement, a late fee of [1.5%/month or flat amount] has been applied to the
> outstanding balance. The updated total due is [new amount]. This fee continues to accrue
> at [rate] per [period] until the balance is paid in full."

**Important**: Only apply late fees if they were in your original contract or invoice.
Retroactively adding them without prior agreement is not legally enforceable and will
inflame the situation.

## Credit Reporting Mention (B2B)

For business clients (not consumers), you can mention commercial credit reporting:

> "Please note that outstanding commercial debts may be reported to business credit
> bureaus, which can affect your company's credit rating and future financing."

Do not use this language for individual freelance clients or consumers — consumer credit
reporting has strict legal requirements (FCRA in the US) and requires specific credentials.
