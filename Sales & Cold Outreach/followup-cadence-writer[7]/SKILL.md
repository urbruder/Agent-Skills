# Follow-Up Cadence Writer

Write the 7 follow-ups that actually get replies — sequenced, angled, and ready to send for your specific deal.

## Core Philosophy

Most people send one follow-up ("Just checking in!") and give up. The people who close deals send 5–9 touches before the prospect responds — but each touch has to earn the right to exist. "Checking in" is not a reason to email someone. **New angle, new value, or new context is.**

The goal of a follow-up sequence is never to nag. It's to give the prospect a genuinely new reason to reply at each touch — and to make it progressively easier to say yes, reschedule, or even say no (clarity beats silence).

Key principles:
- **Every email needs a job** — not "stay top of mind," but a specific function: add value, shift frame, create urgency, make it easy to say no, or close cleanly
- **Shorter over time** — Email 1 can be medium length. By Email 5, you're at 2–3 sentences. The breakup email is 4 lines.
- **Different angle every time** — Never resend the same message. Change the lens: new proof point, related problem, industry context, simple question, or honest admission
- **Make it easy to reply "no"** — A no reply is infinitely more useful than silence. Some emails should explicitly invite a no.
- **Tone shifts across the sequence** — Starts peer-to-peer curious → becomes direct → ends human and honest

## Step 1: Gather Context

Before writing, extract from what the user shares:

**Required (ask if missing):**
1. **Where in the cycle did they go silent?** (cold outreach with no reply / post-discovery call / after sending proposal / mid-negotiation / other)
2. **What was the last interaction?** (what was said, what was sent, what happened)
3. **What are you selling?** (product, service, price range if known)
4. **Who is the prospect?** (title, company type, industry)

**Optional but valuable:**
- Any signals of interest shown before silence (clicked links, attended demo, gave positive vibes on a call)
- Any reason given for going quiet ("I'll get back to you next week" → they didn't)
- Anything known about their situation (busy period, internal process, budget cycle)
- The seller's communication channel preference (email only / LinkedIn OK / text if relationship warrants)

If the user shares their last email or call notes, read them carefully — the follow-up sequence should continue the thread, not restart it.

**If almost nothing is provided:** Write a universal-fit sequence using reasonable defaults for B2B sales and flag where to personalize.

---

## Step 2: Diagnose the Silence

Before writing, silently identify which silence type this is — it shapes the sequence strategy.

| Silence Type | Signal | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Cold ghost** | Never replied to cold outreach | Multi-angle value sequence; permission close at end |
| **Warm ghost** | Was engaged, then disappeared | Acknowledge the shift; add value; make no easy |
| **Post-proposal ghost** | Got proposal, went quiet | Assumption close; address likely objection; breakup |
| **Post-meeting ghost** | Had a good call, then nothing | Call back the energy; surface the blocker; be direct |
| **Re-engagement** | Deal was paused weeks/months ago | Acknowledge the gap; new context hook; low-pressure re-open |
| **Internal champion gone quiet** | Your contact isn't responding but deal may be alive | Route around; reconnect on a different angle |

Use this diagnosis internally to tune the sequence. Don't explain the diagnosis to the user.

## Step 3: Write the 7-Touch Sequence

Each email gets:
- **Touch #** and **timing**
- **Subject line** (or note if it's a reply-thread continuation)
- **The angle / job of this email** (1 sentence — internal note, don't include in output)
- **Full email body** — ready to send
- **Channel note** if a touch works better on LinkedIn/phone/text vs email

---

### The 7-Touch Framework

#### Touch 1 — Day 2–3: The Soft Re-Open
**Job:** Re-engage without pressure. Low-key. Assumes good intent (they're busy, not ignoring you).

- Very short (3–5 sentences)
- Reference the last interaction specifically
- Add one small new thing (an insight, a relevant link, a question)
- No guilt. No "just following up."

**Opening to avoid:** "Just wanted to follow up on my last email..."
**Opening to use:** Something specific. "Saw [thing]. Made me think of what you mentioned about [their goal]."

---

#### Touch 2 — Day 5–7: The Value Add
**Job:** Deliver something genuinely useful, no strings attached. Positions you as helpful, not needy.

- Share a relevant resource, case study, stat, or insight specific to their industry/problem
- Frame it as "thought this might be useful" not "here's why you should buy"
- End with a light CTA: a question, not a meeting request

**Format options:**
- "Came across [article/data] — relevant to the [problem they mentioned]"
- "Quick thought on [their situation] — [1–2 sentence insight]"
- "We just published [resource] — thought of you given [their specific context]"

---

#### Touch 3 — Day 10–12: The Different Angle
**Job:** Reframe the conversation. Come in from a different direction — new use case, different stakeholder angle, adjacent problem.

- Don't repeat what you said before
- Pick the angle least likely to have been your original pitch
- Can be slightly longer (5–7 sentences) — you're making a new case, not nudging

**Reframe approaches:**
- "I was thinking about your situation differently..."
- "Something I didn't mention on our call / in my last email..."
- "We've been working with [similar company type] on [different but related problem]..."

---

#### Touch 4 — Day 15–18: The Social Proof Touch
**Job:** Reduce perceived risk. Make the prospect feel like peers they respect have already done this.

- Lead with a relevant proof point: customer result, company name, outcome metric
- Keep it brief — 1–2 sentence story, not a case study
- End with: "Worth seeing if there's a similar opportunity for [their company]?"

**Avoid:** Generic "our clients see great results!" 
**Use:** Specific. "A [similar company type] we work with [specific result] in [timeframe]."

---

#### Touch 5 — Day 22–25: The Direct Ask
**Job:** Be honest. You've followed up multiple times. Acknowledge it, without apologizing for it.

- 3–4 sentences max
- Name the situation plainly: "I've reached out a few times"
- Ask the direct question: are they still interested, or has something changed?
- Give explicit permission to say no — this is the key move. "A no is totally fine." means it.

**This is the email that often gets the reply** — because it gives the prospect emotional permission to close the loop, which they've been avoiding because they didn't want to say no.

---

#### Touch 6 — Day 30–35: The New Context / Last Real Try
**Job:** Give the sequence one more genuine reason to exist — a new development, a timely hook, a question you haven't asked yet.

- Use a real hook if available: their company news, industry change, seasonal relevance, your own news
- If no hook available: pivot to a simple, specific question that requires a short answer ("Are you still the right person for this, or has this moved to someone else?")
- Signals you're still paying attention, not just running a drip

---

#### Touch 7 — Day 40–45: The Breakup
**Job:** Close the loop cleanly. Give them a graceful out. Leave the relationship intact for the future.

- 4–5 lines maximum
- No guilt, no passive aggression, no "I guess you're not interested"
- Honest and human — "I'll leave the ball in your court"
- Leave a door open — explicitly. Future timing might be different.
- This email often gets replies because it has no pressure at all

**Template structure:**
> Hey [name] — I've reached out a few times and haven't heard back. I completely understand — timing is everything, and this might just not be the moment.
> 
> I'll stop following up so I don't clutter your inbox. But if things shift and [relevant trigger — e.g., "you're revisiting this in Q4" / "the project moves forward"], I'd genuinely love to reconnect.
> 
> Wishing [company] well in the meantime.
> 
> — [Name]

## Step 4: Subject Line Strategy

**For cold sequences:** Each email is a new subject line (prospect never replied, so no thread)
- Keep under 6 words, lowercase
- Rotate styles: question → observation → name drop → curiosity gap

**For warm/post-meeting sequences:** Emails 1–4 are reply-thread continuations (Re: [original subject])
- Only break thread at Touch 5 or 6 when shifting strategy significantly
- Breaking thread = new subject, fresh start, signals something has changed

**Subject line rotation examples:**
```
Touch 1: re: [original thread] (reply continuation)
Touch 2: re: [original thread]
Touch 3: [new angle subject] — break thread here if tone is shifting
Touch 4: quick question
Touch 5: still on your radar?
Touch 6: [company-specific hook or "a thought on [their problem]"]
Touch 7: closing the loop
```

## Step 5: Channel Mix (Optional)

If the user is open to multi-channel, note which touches work well off email:

| Touch | Channel option |
|---|---|
| Touch 2 (value add) | LinkedIn message — feels more casual/helpful |
| Touch 4 (social proof) | LinkedIn + email — share proof publicly then reference it |
| Touch 5 (direct ask) | Phone call or voicemail — directness lands harder verbally |
| Touch 6 (new context) | LinkedIn comment on their content → then email the same day |
| Touch 7 (breakup) | Email only — needs to be in writing, not spoken |

Note to user if they haven't mentioned channel preferences: "These are all written as emails — want me to adapt any of them for LinkedIn DMs or phone scripts?"

## Sequence Variations by Silence Type

### Post-Proposal Ghost (highest stakes)
- Touch 1: Assumption close — "I'm going to assume no news means you're still reviewing — happy to answer any questions that came up"
- Touch 3: Surface the likely objection — price, timing, internal approval, comparison shopping
- Touch 5: Offer a decision-helper — revised scope option, extended deadline, pilot/starter version
- Touch 7: Withdraw the offer cleanly — proposal expires, hold their slot, be human about it

### Re-Engagement (deal went cold weeks/months ago)
- Skip the first 2 touches — they know you
- Lead with: new context hook (their company news, relevant industry change, or your own new offering)
- Sequence is 4–5 touches, not 7 — respect the relationship
- Tone is warmer, more peer-level — you've met

### Internal Champion Gone Quiet
- Touch 3: "Is this still moving forward on your end, or has the internal process shifted?"
- Touch 5: Route around — try a different stakeholder at the company (flag this as a decision for the user)
- Touch 6: Make it easy for them to hand off — "If it's moved to someone else, happy to connect directly with them"

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- ❌ "Just checking in" — no reason to reply
- ❌ "I wanted to follow up on my previous email" — restates the obvious
- ❌ Sending the same email twice with "Re:" — they know
- ❌ Guilt-tripping ("I've sent several emails now...") — creates defensiveness
- ❌ Asking for a meeting in every single touch — exhausting
- ❌ Long emails past Touch 3 — attention drops with every non-reply
- ❌ Negative breakup ("I guess this isn't a priority for you") — burns the bridge
- ❌ Vague value adds ("thought you might find this interesting") — say why it's relevant
- ❌ More than 7 touches without a major new development — that's spam

**Loading into a sequencer?**

**Personalization flags:**
Short list of spots to customize before activating — specific names, proof points, links. Keep to 3–5 items max.

## Quality Check

Before outputting, verify:
- [ ] Does every touch have a distinct angle — no two emails make the same point?
- [ ] Do emails get shorter as the sequence progresses?
- [ ] Does Touch 5 explicitly give permission to say no?
- [ ] Is Touch 7 warm and bridge-preserving, not passive aggressive?
- [ ] Is there at least one touch that delivers value with no ask attached?
- [ ] Could every email stand alone — does it make sense without reading the previous ones?
- [ ] Are subject lines varied in format across the sequence?
- [ ] Is the whole sequence completable with real copy that works today?
