# ICP & Messaging Builder

Defines your ideal buyer with precision and generates the exact words — hooks, pain points, value angles — that make them stop and pay attention.

---

## Step 1: Extract What They've Got

Gather context from what the user provides. Look for:

| Input | Where to find it |
|---|---|
| **What they sell** | Product/service description, website, their own words |
| **Current customers** | "We work with..." / examples they mention |
| **Problem they solve** | May be implicit — infer if not stated |
| **Who they think their buyer is** | Often too broad ("SMBs", "marketers") |
| **What's not working** | Generic messaging, low conversion, wrong leads |
| **Competitor context** | Who else plays here |
| **Deal size / sales motion** | Self-serve, transactional, enterprise |

**If they give you a product description and nothing else:** That's enough to start. Build the ICP from first principles and show your work. Don't ask 10 questions — make smart inferences and state them.

**If they've shared existing messaging:** Use it as raw material. Identify what's vague, generic, or feature-focused, and sharpen it.

---

## Step 2: ICP Definition

Build the ICP across four layers:

### Layer 1 — Firmographic (B2B) / Demographic (B2C)

**B2B:**
- Company size (employees, revenue range)
- Industry / vertical
- Business model (SaaS, services, e-commerce, etc.)
- Geography (if relevant)
- Growth stage (pre-revenue, Series A, scaling, enterprise)
- Tech stack signals (if applicable)

**B2C:**
- Age range, life stage
- Income / spending behavior
- Geographic or cultural context
- Channel where they're reachable

### Layer 2 — Situational Triggers (the "why now")

The conditions that make someone ready to buy *right now* — not just "companies that could benefit" but the specific situation that creates urgency.

Examples:
- Just raised a round (hiring pressure, growth mode)
- Just hit a growth ceiling with their current solution
- Regulatory change forcing action
- New hire in the decision-making role
- Recent bad experience with a competitor
- Specific operational pain hit a threshold

**This is the highest-leverage layer.** ICP without triggers = a list of companies. ICP with triggers = a list of companies with a reason to call them this week.

### Layer 3 — Psychographic (who the buyer actually is)

- **Role** — exact title, not just "marketing"
- **What they're measured on** — their KPIs, their boss's expectations
- **What keeps them up at night** — not feature-level pain, existential-level pain
- **How they make decisions** — fast/intuitive vs. committee vs. data-driven
- **What they're afraid of** — looking bad, wasting budget, making the wrong call
- **What they want to be seen as** — innovative, responsible, strategic, efficient

### Layer 4 — Negative ICP (who to stop chasing)

Define who is *not* the ICP — companies or buyers that look right but aren't:
- Wrong company stage (too early / too late)
- Wrong buying motivation (shopping for price, not value)
- Wrong internal champion (influencer, not decision-maker)
- Deal economics that don't work
- Segment that churns, complains, or doesn't expand

## Step 3: Messaging Architecture

With the ICP locked, build messaging across five elements:

### 1. The Hook (opening line / attention-grabber)

A single sentence that makes the right person think "that's exactly my problem."

**Formula options:**
- **The Named Pain:** "[Specific person] spends [X time/money] on [specific painful thing] every [week/month/quarter]."
- **The Before/After:** "Most [ICP] are still [old way]. [Company] lets you [new way]."
- **The Challenger:** "[Common belief about the problem] is wrong. Here's what's actually happening."
- **The Specific Result:** "[ICP] use [Product] to [specific outcome] in [timeframe]."

Rules:
- Name the buyer explicitly (not "teams" or "businesses" — name the role or situation)
- Reference a specific, felt pain (not a category)
- No adjectives like "powerful", "seamless", "best-in-class"
- Short enough to say in one breath

### 2. Pain Points (in buyer language)

Three to five pain points, written the way the buyer would say them in a conversation — not how a marketer would describe them.

**Marketer language (bad):** "Inefficient manual processes reduce team productivity"  
**Buyer language (good):** "I'm paying three people to do something that should take 20 minutes"

For each pain point:
- The surface complaint (what they say)
- The underlying fear (what it means if it doesn't get fixed)
- The business cost (what it's actually costing them)

### 3. Value Angles (what to lead with for different contexts)

Different situations call for different angles. Build 2–3:

| Angle | Lead with | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| **Efficiency** | Time/cost saved | Ops-focused buyers, tight budgets |
| **Growth** | Revenue/pipeline impact | Sales, marketing, founder |
| **Risk reduction** | What they stop worrying about | Risk-averse buyers, compliance |
| **Status/credibility** | What it signals to their team/boss | New hires, ambitious mid-level |

### 4. Objection Inoculations

The top 2–3 objections this buyer will have — and the one-line reframe for each.

Format:
> **Objection:** "[What they'll say]"  
> **What it really means:** [The underlying concern]  
> **Reframe:** "[What to say back]"

### 5. Proof Requirements

What evidence this specific buyer needs before they'll trust you:
- Social proof type (peer companies, recognizable names, case studies, numbers)
- Specific metrics they care about (time saved, revenue lifted, churn reduced)
- Risk reducers they'll want (free trial, pilot, money-back, security review)

## Step 5: Sharpen If It's Too Broad

If the user's ICP is still too wide after the first pass, push them to narrow with these prompts:

**"Who's your best customer right now?"**  
The real ICP is usually hiding in the 2–3 customers who closed fastest, paid most, and churned least. Build the profile from them backward.

**"Who do you *not* want to sell to?"**  
Defining the negative ICP often forces more precision than defining the positive one.

**"What had to be true for your last 3 wins to happen?"**  
Trigger conditions come from this question.

**"If you could only target one company size / industry / role — which would it be?"**  
Forces prioritization. They can always expand later. Starting too broad means standing for nothing.

---

## Step 6: Offer Follow-Ons

After the ICP & messaging brief, offer:

- **Channel-specific copy** — take the messaging and write it for LinkedIn, cold email, paid ads, or a landing page
- **Cold DM sequence** — use the hook and pain points to build outreach (pairs with cold-dm-sequence skill)
- **Battlecard vs. top competitor** — how this positioning stacks up against who they're losing to
- **Discovery question set** — questions to qualify against the ICP in sales calls
- **One-pager / pitch deck slide** — the ICP & messaging as a formatted leave-behind

---

## Craft Standards

**Avoid at all costs:**
- "Businesses of all sizes" — not an ICP
- "Decision-makers" — not a role
- "Powerful", "seamless", "innovative", "best-in-class" — not messaging
- Pain points written from your perspective, not the buyer's
- Hooks that could describe any company in the category

**Signs the messaging is landing:**
- The buyer reads the hook and says "how did you know?"
- Pain points use words the buyer would actually use in conversation
- The value angle addresses what the buyer is *measured on*, not just what they want
- The objection reframes don't dismiss the concern — they reframe it
