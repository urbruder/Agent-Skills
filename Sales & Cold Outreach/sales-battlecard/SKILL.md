# Sales Battlecard Builder

Produces a rep-ready competitive battlecard: the exact words to say when a prospect brings up a competitor, the landmines to plant, and the proof to close with — all on one page a rep can reference mid-call.

---

## Step 1: Intake

Gather from context or ask (only what's missing):

| Input | Why |
|---|---|
| **Your product/company** | What's being sold |
| **The competitor** | Who the card is against |
| **Your known strengths vs. them** | What you believe you win on |
| **Where you lose to them** | Honest weaknesses — cards that ignore this fail in the field |
| **Common objections when they come up** | "We're already using X", "X is cheaper", "X is more established" |
| **Your best win stories against them** | Real proof points reps can reference |
| **Who the buyer is** | Affects which angles matter most |

If the user gives you a product/competitor pair and limited detail, do research-informed inference and state your assumptions. A partial card built quickly is more useful than no card.

---

## Step 2: Research Mode (if needed)

If you have web search available and the competitor is a real, known company:
- Look up their positioning, pricing page, and G2/Capterra reviews
- Find what customers praise and complain about — this is gold for battlecard differentiation
- Check their LinkedIn job postings (signals where they're investing and where they're weak)
- Look for recent news (funding, layoffs, product changes) — reps can use this live

Surface what you find as inputs into the card — cite sources where relevant so reps can verify.

---

## Step 3: Battlecard Architecture

A battlecard has six sections. Every section should be written in the voice of a rep speaking to a prospect — not a marketing deck.

### Section 1: The 30-Second Positioning Snapshot
One paragraph. Answers: "How do I think about this competitor in 10 seconds?"

Format:
- Who they are and who they're really built for
- Their genuine strengths (acknowledge them — reps who dismiss the competitor lose credibility)
- The core thing they can't do / aren't built for
- The one-line "we win when..." summary

### Section 2: When to Use This Card
Specific trigger situations that indicate this competitor is in the deal:
- Prospect mentions them by name
- Prospect is already a customer ("we use X for Y")
- Prospect asks about a feature this competitor is known for
- Prospect came from a company that standardized on this competitor

### Section 3: The Trap Questions
3–5 questions reps ask the prospect that naturally surface where the competitor is weak — without directly attacking them. The prospect reaches the conclusion themselves.

Format per question:
> **Ask:** "[Question text]"  
> **Why:** What it surfaces  
> **If they say X:** Follow-up or transition

These are the most valuable part of the card. Reps who ask great questions don't need to pitch — the competitor disqualifies itself.

### Section 4: Head-to-Head (The Honest Matrix)

A short, honest comparison. Three columns: **We win**, **They win**, **It depends**.

Rules:
- "We win" = genuine, defensible advantages — not marketing spin
- "They win" = real areas where the competitor is stronger; reps need to know this so they're not caught flat-footed
- "It depends" = where the outcome comes down to the specific buyer's situation

Do NOT list everything. 4–6 rows max. Only what matters in a competitive conversation.

### Section 5: Objection Scripts

The 3–4 things a prospect actually says when this competitor is in the deal — and the exact response.

Format per objection:
> **They say:** "[Prospect's words]"  
> **What they mean:** [Underlying concern — not always what they said]  
> **Say this:** "[Rep's response — word for word]"  
> **Then:** [Next move — ask a question, offer proof, transition]

Rules for the scripts:
- Write them in spoken English — contractions, natural rhythm
- Never dismiss the competitor outright ("they're terrible" loses credibility)
- Acknowledge, reframe, redirect — the classic structure that actually works
- End each script with a question or a next step, not a statement

### Section 6: Proof & Landmines

**Proof to deploy:**
- 1–3 specific win stories against this competitor (company type + result — not full case studies)
- Key metrics that differentiate (where you have them)
- Any third-party validation (reviews, analyst mentions, awards)

**Landmines to plant early:**
- Questions or topics to raise in discovery that pre-empt the competitor's strengths
- Things to establish about your approach before the competitor gets to make their pitch
- "Before you evaluate anyone, make sure you ask about [X]" — things that favor you

## Step 5: Card Quality Standards

**Trap questions** are the hardest part to write and the most valuable. A good trap question:
- Sounds neutral and curious, not leading
- Surfaces a real problem with the competitor without naming them
- Puts the discovery of the problem in the prospect's own words
- Can't be answered "yes and everything is fine" — it opens a thread

Bad: "Have you had issues with [Competitor's] customer support?"  
Good: "When something goes wrong on a Friday afternoon, what does your escalation path look like with your current vendor?"

**Objection scripts** must pass the "would a human actually say this" test:
- Read them aloud — if they sound like a product brochure, rewrite them
- They should acknowledge what's true about the competitor before pivoting
- Every script ends with a question or a concrete next step

**The honest matrix** must include real "They win" items:
- Cards that only list your advantages are ignored by reps who've been in deals
- Knowing where you lose helps reps disqualify deals they shouldn't be in
- It builds trust: reps who can say "honestly, if X is critical for you, [Competitor] might be the right call" win deals on credibility

---

## Step 6: Offer Follow-Ons

After the battlecard, offer:

- **Email template** — what to send when a prospect mentions the competitor in writing
- **Discovery question set** — a fuller list of qualifying questions that bake in competitive positioning
- **Executive version** — a shorter, higher-level card for deals where the competitor relationship is at the C-suite
- **Multi-competitor comparison** — if they're losing to several players, a side-by-side positioning overview
- **Enablement brief** — a short document explaining the card to sales managers for team training

## Analyst Notes: What Makes Battlecards Fail in the Field

**Too long.** A rep on a call will not scroll through 4 pages. One page. If it doesn't fit, cut.

**Written for the marketer, not the rep.** Feature lists, capability matrices, and "we're best-in-class" language gets ignored. Write it the way a senior rep talks to a prospect.

**No honest weaknesses.** Reps know where they lose. If the card pretends otherwise, they stop trusting it.

**No specific proof.** "Customers love us" is useless. "A 200-person logistics company switched from [Competitor] after losing $40K in a billing dispute they couldn't resolve" is a rep's best friend.

**Trap questions that are obviously traps.** If the question sounds like an attack, prospects get defensive and the competitor gets sympathy. Neutral tone, genuine curiosity.

**Not updated.** Competitors change pricing, launch features, run promotions. A card from 18 months ago might be actively harmful. Add a "last updated" date and a note about when to refresh.
