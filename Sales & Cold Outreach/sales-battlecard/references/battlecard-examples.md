# Battlecard Examples

Two complete worked battlecards across different competitive scenarios.

---

## Example 1: B2B SaaS — Project Management Tool vs. a Dominant Incumbent

**You:** Newer, focused project management tool for creative agencies  
**Competitor:** A large, well-known general-purpose PM platform  
**Buyer:** Agency Operations Director or Founder, 20–100 person agency

---

```
WHO THEY ARE (10-second version)
BigPM is a genuinely good tool — it works, it's stable, and half their
prospects already use it. It's built for any team doing any kind of work,
which is exactly the problem. Agencies have client billing, project 
profitability, creative review cycles, and resource utilization — none 
of which BigPM handles natively. Teams end up running BigPM plus 2–3 
other tools just to get a complete picture. We win when the agency is 
tired of duct-taping their ops stack together.

• Prospect says "we use BigPM" or "we're evaluating BigPM"
• Prospect asks about integrations with their billing/finance tool
• Prospect comes from a non-agency background and defaults to "we use what

• Deal involves an agency that's grown past 20 people and outgrown their 
  current setup

TRAP QUESTIONS (ask these early)

1. "How do you currently track whether a project came in over or under budget 
   — like at the end of a job, do you know your actual margin?"
   → Surfaces: BigPM has no native profitability tracking; most agencies 
     are doing this in Excel after the fact
   → If they say "we do it in spreadsheets": "How long does that take you 
     each month, roughly?"

2. "When a client wants to review and approve creative, where does that 
   happen for you today?"
   → Surfaces: BigPM has no creative review/proofing workflow; most 
     agencies use a separate tool (or email)
   → If they say "email or a separate tool": "Does it ever cause version 
     confusion or delay approvals?"

3. "If I asked you right now which of your active projects are at risk of 
   going over hours — how quickly could you tell me?"
   → Surfaces: Real-time resource/hours visibility is weak in BigPM without 
     custom setup
   → If they hesitate: "That's actually the number one thing agency ops 
     leaders tell us they can't see until it's too late."

OBJECTION SCRIPTS

▸ "We already use BigPM and the team is used to it."
  What they mean: Switching cost feels high; don't want to re-train everyone
  Say: "Totally makes sense — switching is a real cost. The question I'd ask 
  is: what are you doing outside of BigPM to run the business? Most agencies 
  we talk to are running 2–3 other tools alongside it for billing, proofing, 
  and reporting. If that's your situation, the switching cost might be lower 
  than the ongoing friction cost."
  Then: "What tools are you running alongside BigPM right now?"

▸ "BigPM has way more integrations."
  What they mean: I'm worried about losing connections to tools we already use
  Say: "They definitely do — 1,000+ is hard to beat. The question is which 
  ones you actually need. Most agencies live in about 5–8 tools. Which ones 
  are non-negotiable for you? I can tell you upfront if we cover them."
  Then: Walk through their core stack — you almost certainly cover it

▸ "BigPM is cheaper per seat."
  What they mean: Budget sensitivity; needs CFO or founder justification
  Say: "Per seat, yes. But I'd push back on the total cost picture — if you're 
  also paying for a proofing tool, a time tracker, and doing manual margin 
  reports, the all-in number is usually higher. Want to do a quick comparison 
  on total stack cost?"
  Then: Offer a 15-minute stack audit call

▸ "I've heard the onboarding is complicated."
  What they mean: Fear of a failed rollout; been burned before
  Say: "That's fair to check on. We have a 14-day agency onboarding track — 
  most teams are live in week one. I'd rather show you than tell you — want 
  me to connect you with an agency your size that went through it last quarter?"
  Then: Reference customer intro

PROOF TO DEPLOY

Win stories:
• 45-person design agency switched from BigPM → cut monthly reporting from 
  6 hours to 45 minutes; identified 3 unprofitable retainer clients in 
  first 30 days
• 30-person integrated agency replaced BigPM + Harvest + InVision with us 
  → reduced SaaS spend by $2,200/month

Third-party: 4.7 on G2 for "agency use case" reviews vs. BigPM's 4.2 
in same category filter

LANDMINES TO PLANT EARLY

Before BigPM gets positioned as "the safe choice," establish:
• "One thing we always recommend: before evaluating any tool, map out 
  your full ops stack — a lot of teams undercount what they're actually 
  running alongside their PM tool."
• Ask about project profitability visibility in discovery — if they can't 
  answer it, you've opened the door before they ever think about BigPM
• "The agencies we see struggle most with tool consolidation are the ones 
  who picked a general-purpose tool and then bolted on everything else — 
  worth asking any vendor how much of your workflow lives natively in 
  their product vs. requires integrations."
```

## Example 2: Services Business — Recruiting Firm vs. a Larger Generalist Competitor

**You:** Boutique technical recruiting firm, 10-person team, specializes in Series A–C engineering hires  
**Competitor:** Large national staffing firm with a tech practice  
**Buyer:** Engineering Manager, VP Eng, or Head of People at a startup

---

```
COMPETITIVE BATTLECARD
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
You: [TalentFocus]  vs.  Them: [BigStaff National]
For: VPs of Eng / Heads of People at Series A–C startups

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
WHO THEY ARE (10-second version)
BigStaff is a real firm with real reach — they fill roles at scale across 
dozens of industries. That's also their problem. When a startup needs a 
Staff Engineer with distributed systems experience who fits a 25-person 
engineering culture, BigStaff's tech practice is one vertical among twenty. 
Their recruiters work a broad book, move fast, and optimize for throughput. 
We win when quality of fit matters more than volume of candidates.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
WHEN THIS CARD IS LIVE
• Prospect has used a large generalist firm before and is "evaluating options"
• Prospect mentions BigStaff by name in their vendor review
• Prospect is shopping primarily on fee percentage
• Deal is for a technical role where the hiring manager will interview 
  candidates personally (senior IC or leadership)

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
TRAP QUESTIONS

1. "With your last external recruiting partner — out of every 10 candidates 
   they submitted, how many made it past the first technical screen?"
   → Surfaces: Generalist firms often have low pass-through rates on technical 
     roles; hiring managers waste time on unqualified candidates
   → If they say "maybe 2–3": "So you were doing the screening work for them, 
     essentially. That's pretty common with generalist firms."

2. "How important is it that the recruiter actually understands the technical 
   role — like, could you talk to them about the architecture problem you're 
   trying to solve with this hire?"
   → Surfaces: Generalist recruiters can't have technical credibility 
     conversations with candidates or hiring managers
   → If they say "very important": "That's actually where we built our model 
     differently — every recruiter on our team has either worked in engineering 
     or spent 5+ years exclusively in technical recruiting."

3. "What's your biggest fear about this hire going wrong?"
   → Surfaces: For startup hires, culture fit and technical depth matter more 
     than speed; opens conversation about quality vs. volume tradeoff
   → This question reframes the conversation from "who is cheapest" to 
     "what does failure cost"

OBJECTION SCRIPTS

▸ "BigStaff has a larger candidate database."
  What they mean: More options = better chances of filling the role
  Say: "They definitely have more volume. The question is whether more 
  resumes means more relevant resumes for a technical startup role. 
  Database size matters more for high-volume, lower-specificity hiring. 
  For a Staff Engineer who needs to fit your architecture and your culture, 
  the quality of the sourcer matters more than the size of the database. 
  What does your typical screening process look like after you get 
  candidates from a firm?"
  Then: Pivot to pass-through rate conversation

▸ "Their fee is 2% lower."
  What they mean: Budget pressure; needs to justify to finance or founder
  Say: "Fair — 2% is real money. Here's how I'd think about it: if we get 
  you to offer in 6 weeks instead of 14, and your candidate pass-through 
  rate is 60% instead of 20%, what's that worth in engineering productivity 
  and your own time? I'm not saying we're always the right call on budget — 
  but the fee comparison usually looks different when you factor in total 
  time-to-hire and hiring manager hours."
  Then: Offer to walk through a cost-of-vacancy estimate

▸ "We've used BigStaff before and it was fine."
  What they mean: Low switching motivation; "fine" is good enough
  Say: "That's actually the most useful thing you could tell me — what 
  made it 'fine' vs. great? A lot of companies use both of us for different 
  role types. If the last hire was a generalist PM role, BigStaff is probably 
  the right call. If this one is a technical IC or engineering leader, that's 
  where we're built differently. What's the role you're trying to fill?"
  Then: Qualify whether the role is actually in our sweet spot

PROOF TO DEPLOY

Win stories:
• Series B fintech (40 engineers) tried BigStaff for 3 months on a Staff 
  Eng search, got 18 resumes, 2 passed technical screen. Came to us, 
  filled in 5 weeks, hire is still there 2 years later.
• Head of People at a Series A replaced their staffing vendor after 
  calculating she was spending 8 hours/week screening resumes. With us, 
  she reviews 4–6 pre-screened candidates per role.

Key number: Average technical pass-through rate: 58% vs. industry average 
of ~22% for generalist firms (based on client feedback)

Before BigStaff gets positioned as "more resources = better":
• In discovery, always ask "what did your pass-through rate look like with 
  your last recruiting partner?" — if they don't know, teach them to ask 
  for it; this becomes an evaluation criterion that favors us
• "One thing worth asking any firm: can I talk to the actual recruiter 
  who will be sourcing for me, and can they speak to the technical 
  requirements of the role?"
• Establish early: "We work best as a focused partner on 1–3 roles at a 
  time rather than a volume vendor — if that model fits, great; if you 
  need to fill 15 roles in 60 days, I'll tell you upfront we're not the 
  right call."
```
