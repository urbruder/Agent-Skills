# Reply-Handling Playbook

Paste a tricky prospect reply. Get 3 reframes that move the deal forward. Never freeze on a hard email again.

## Core Philosophy

Every difficult prospect reply is one of a small number of patterns in disguise. "Too expensive" is usually about ROI clarity or timing, not price. "Not a priority" often means "I don't have internal buy-in yet." "Send me some information" frequently means "I'm not ready to talk but I don't want to be rude." 

The skill isn't to overcome objections — it's to **decode what the reply actually means** and give the seller 3 strategically different ways to respond, each with a different goal and a different relationship risk. The seller chooses the one that fits their read on the prospect.

Key principles:
- **Diagnose before drafting** — the right response depends on what the reply is actually signaling, not what it literally says
- **3 options, 3 different strategies** — not 3 versions of the same tone, but 3 genuinely different moves
- **Short responses win** — most reply emails should be 3–6 sentences. Under 100 words almost always.
- **Keep the door open** — even "no" replies deserve a response that preserves the relationship
- **Never argue** — reframe, don't defend. Defending triggers resistance; reframing opens a new path

---

## Step 1: Read the Reply

Accept the prospect's reply as pasted text. Read it fully.

If the user also provides context (their original email, the deal stage, what they're selling, who the prospect is) — use it. If not, infer from the reply itself. Don't ask for context unless the reply is genuinely uninterpretable without it.

---

## Step 2: Diagnose the Reply Type

Classify the reply into one of these patterns. More than one can apply — identify the primary and secondary signal.

### The 12 Reply Types

**1. The Price Objection**
*Signals:* "too expensive", "over budget", "can't justify the cost", "cheaper alternatives exist", "need a discount"
*What it usually means:* ROI isn't clear enough, timing is wrong, or they're using price to test flexibility

**2. The Timing Deflection**
*Signals:* "not the right time", "check back in Q3/next year", "too busy right now", "we're in a freeze"
*What it usually means:* Genuinely bad timing, OR they lack internal buy-in and are stalling, OR they're not the real decision-maker

**3. The Priority Objection**
*Signals:* "not a priority right now", "we have bigger fish to fry", "on the back burner"
*What it usually means:* They don't yet see the cost of inaction, or this is delegated down and has no internal champion

**4. The Brush-Off / Information Request**
*Signals:* "send me some information", "send over details", "put something in writing"
*What it usually means:* Polite rejection they don't want to say directly, OR genuine curiosity with low urgency, OR they want to vet you before committing to a call

**5. The Competitor Deflection**
*Signals:* "we already use [competitor]", "we're happy with our current solution", "we're locked into a contract"
*What it usually means:* Status quo bias, switching cost fear, or genuine satisfaction — but rarely 100% satisfaction

**6. The "Not Interested" Flat No**
*Signals:* "not interested", "please remove me", "this isn't relevant to us"
*What it usually means:* Wrong timing, wrong person, or wrong message — but occasionally a firm no worth accepting

**7. The Stakeholder Deflection**
*Signals:* "not my decision", "need to run it by my team", "you'd need to talk to [other person]"
*What it usually means:* They're not the buyer, OR they're interested but need cover to move forward, OR they're testing whether you'll do the legwork

**8. The Vague Positive**
*Signals:* "interesting, I'll keep you in mind", "sounds good, let's reconnect sometime", "I'll think about it"
*What it usually means:* Polite non-commitment — they need a reason to move from "interesting" to "yes"

**9. The Negotiation Signal**
*Signals:* "what's the best you can do", "any flexibility on pricing", "what if we did less scope"
*What it usually means:* They want to buy — they're just testing the deal terms

**10. The Specific Objection**
*Signals:* A specific, concrete concern — "your platform doesn't integrate with X", "we need Y feature", "your team doesn't have experience in Z"
*What it usually means:* A real blocker that needs a real answer, or a test to see if you'll engage substantively

**11. The Internal Process Stall**
*Signals:* "waiting for budget approval", "need sign-off from legal/finance/leadership", "going through procurement"
*What it usually means:* Genuine process friction — they want to move but can't unilaterally

**12. The Confused / Misaligned Reply**
*Signals:* They misunderstood the offer, are responding to the wrong thing, or seem to be talking about a different product/problem
*What it usually means:* The original message was unclear, or they're a different buyer persona than targeted

## Step 3: Generate 3 Strategic Responses

For each tricky reply, produce exactly 3 responses. Each must:
- Have a distinct **strategic label** (e.g., "Reframe the ROI", "Make the no easy", "Surface the real blocker")
- Have a distinct **goal** — they should not all try to do the same thing
- Be **complete and send-ready** — not a template with brackets everywhere
- Be **short** — 3–6 sentences as a default. Never more than 8.

### The Response Strategy Menu

Pull 3 from this menu based on the reply type. Choose strategies that are genuinely different from each other — not just different tones of the same move.

---

**REFRAME THE ROI**
Don't defend the price. Reframe what the price buys.
> "The number that usually matters more to [their role] than the cost is [specific outcome metric]. For context, [proof point or calculation]. Does it change the math if [reframe]?"

Best for: Price objection, priority objection

---

**MAKE THE NO EASY**
Give them permission to say no. Paradoxically, this often gets a yes — or at least the real objection.
> "If the timing genuinely isn't right, that's completely fine — no hard feelings. But I want to make sure I'm not missing something: is it timing, or is there a different concern underneath it?"

Best for: Timing deflection, vague positive, brush-off

---

**SURFACE THE REAL BLOCKER**
Stop guessing. Ask the direct question.
> "I want to make sure I'm responding to the right thing — when you say [their exact phrase], is the concern more about [Option A] or [Option B]?"

Best for: Almost any reply — specificity always beats assumption

---

**ANCHOR TO COST OF INACTION**
Shift the frame from cost-to-buy to cost-of-not-buying.
> "Totally understand [their concern]. The thing that gives me pause is [specific cost or risk of doing nothing] — especially given [their context or situation]. Does that feel like a real risk from where you sit?"

Best for: Priority objection, timing deflection, competitor deflection

---

**THE MICRO-COMMITMENT**
Don't ask for the big yes. Ask for a tiny yes that keeps the conversation alive.
> "No pressure on moving forward right now. Would it make sense to put 20 minutes on the calendar for [specific month] — so if timing shifts, we're not starting from zero?"

Best for: Timing deflection, vague positive, internal process stall

---

**ROUTE AROUND**
Acknowledge they're not the decision-maker; ask who is — politely, specifically.
> "That makes sense — happy to take it to whoever owns [relevant decision area]. Who's the best person to loop in, and would an intro from you help or is it better if I reach out directly?"

Best for: Stakeholder deflection

---

**ACCEPT THE NO GRACEFULLY (AND LEAVE THE DOOR OPEN)**
Don't fight it. Agree, be human, and plant a seed for the future.
> "Completely respect that. I won't keep filling your inbox. If [relevant future scenario] — a new product push, different budget cycle, whatever — changes the calculus, I'd love to reconnect. Wishing [company] well in the meantime."

Best for: Flat no, timing deflection (hard version)

---

**ACKNOWLEDGE THE COMPETITOR — THEN DIFFERENTIATE**
Don't trash the competitor. Acknowledge, then ask one question that creates doubt.
> "[Competitor] is solid. A lot of our customers came from there, actually. The question I'd ask is: [one specific capability gap or use case the competitor doesn't serve well]. Is that something that's come up?"

Best for: Competitor deflection

---

**RESPOND TO THE LITERAL REQUEST (STRATEGICALLY)**
If they asked for information, send it — but make it a Trojan horse for a conversation.
> "Happy to send something over — want to make sure it's actually useful. Quick question before I do: [one qualifying question that helps you send the right thing AND gets them to engage]."

Best for: Information request / brush-off

---

**NAME THE DYNAMIC**
Be direct about what's happening. Sometimes the most effective move is to just say it.
> "I want to be honest — it feels like this might not be the right fit right now, and that's okay. I'd rather know than keep guessing. Is that the case?"

Best for: Vague positive, flat no, ambiguous stall

---

**NEGOTIATE FROM STRENGTH**
If they're asking for a deal, give them optionality — not a discount.
> "Happy to look at that. The way I think about it: we could reduce the scope to [Option A] at [lower price], or keep the full scope and adjust payment terms. Which of those fits better?"

Best for: Negotiation signal, price objection (when discount is a real option)

---

**ADDRESS THE SPECIFIC OBJECTION HEAD-ON**
For concrete, specific blockers — give a concrete, specific answer.
> "[Their concern] is a fair point. Here's how we actually handle that: [direct answer]. Does that address it, or is there more to it?"

Best for: Specific objection, confused/misaligned reply

Present the 3 responses in a clean, scannable format:

WHAT'S HAPPENING: [1-sentence diagnosis of what the reply actually signals]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

[When to use this: 1 sentence on the goal and relationship risk]

[Full email, ready to send]

---

OPTION 2 — [Strategy Label]
[When to use this: 1 sentence]

[Full email, ready to send]

---

OPTION 3 — [Strategy Label]
[When to use this: 1 sentence]

The **"WHAT'S HAPPENING"** line is crucial — it shows the seller they're not alone in finding the reply confusing, and it reframes how they read it before they read the responses.

## Step 5: Special Handling for Edge Cases

### Reply is genuinely hostile / rude
Don't match the energy. Option 1 should be calm and professional. Option 2 should be the graceful exit. Option 3 can be the "name the dynamic" approach — direct but not defensive.
Never write a response that defends against the hostility or argues back.

### Reply contains multiple objections
Pick the primary one. Note to the user: "They've raised a few things here — I focused on [X] as the main blocker. If [Y] is actually the bigger concern, let me know and I'll rewrite around that."

### Reply is from someone who isn't the original contact
Flag it: "Looks like this reply came from a different person than you originally contacted. The responses below treat them as the new point of contact." Adjust the responses to introduce context lightly.

### Reply is ambiguous — could mean multiple things
Lean into the ambiguity. Make "Surface the Real Blocker" one of the three options so the seller can choose to just ask.

### Reply in a different language
Respond in the same language as the reply.

### The reply is actually positive but the user is confused
Sometimes a reply is a soft yes and the sender doesn't see it. Diagnose it as such and write 3 responses that capitalize on the opening rather than treating it as a problem.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid in the Responses

- ❌ "Great question!" or "I completely understand!" — sycophantic openers kill credibility
- ❌ Restating the prospect's objection back at them verbatim ("I hear that you feel the price is too high...") — patronizing
- ❌ Asking multiple questions in one reply — one question gets answered; three get ignored
- ❌ Long paragraphs — no response should exceed 8 sentences
- ❌ Defending your product against an objection — defend nothing; reframe everything
- ❌ Fake urgency ("This offer expires Friday!") unless it's genuinely true
- ❌ "No worries!" — too casual; it minimizes the prospect's concern
- ❌ Using the word "actually" to correct them — condescending
- ❌ Starting any email with "I" — starts the reader thinking about you, not them

## Quality Check

Before outputting:
- [ ] Is the diagnosis accurate to what the reply actually signals (not just what it literally says)?
- [ ] Are all 3 options genuinely different strategies — not just different tones?
- [ ] Is every response under 8 sentences?
- [ ] Does at least one option make it easy for the prospect to say no?
- [ ] Are all responses send-ready — no unfilled placeholders?
- [ ] Is there no defensiveness or arguing in any of the responses?
- [ ] Does the "When to use this" guidance help the seller choose without hand-holding?
