# Objection-Crusher Scripts

Build a complete, personalized objection playbook — the 10 objections you hear most, each with 3 scripted responses ready to practice and deploy.

## Core Philosophy

Objections are not rejections. They are requests for more information delivered in a defensive wrapper. "Too expensive" means "I'm not yet convinced of the value." "We already have a solution" means "I don't see enough difference to justify switching." "Not the right time" often means "I haven't made this a priority internally."

The seller who freezes on an objection loses the deal. The seller who has a practiced, natural response keeps the conversation going — and that's all that's required to eventually close.

This skill does two things:
1. **Builds the playbook** — all 10 objections mapped and scripted for the user's specific context
2. **Teaches the framework** — so the seller understands *why* each response works and can adapt it live, not just recite it

The best objection responses share four traits:
- **Acknowledge** without conceding (validate the concern without agreeing it's a dealbreaker)
- **Reframe** without arguing (change the lens, don't fight the lens)
- **Ask** rather than tell (a question keeps the conversation going; a statement invites a counter)
- **Short** — responses under 4 sentences land harder than paragraphs

---

## Step 1: Gather Context

Before building the playbook, collect:

**Required:**
1. **What are you selling?** (product/service, rough price point, B2B or B2C)
2. **Who are you selling to?** (buyer title, company size, industry)
3. **What are the 3–5 objections you hear most?** (if they know — if not, proceed to Step 2 defaults)

**Optional but sharpens everything:**
- How long is your typical sales cycle?
- Where do objections usually surface? (cold outreach / discovery call / proposal stage / close)
- Any objections that have killed deals you thought you'd win?
- What's your biggest differentiator vs. competitors?

If the user can't name their top objections, use the universal top 10 (Step 2) and customize the scripts around their product/context.

**If zero context is given:** Ask one question only — "What are you selling and who to?" — then build the full playbook from that.

---

## Step 2: Identify the 10 Objections

Use the user's stated objections as the foundation. Fill any gaps from this universal top-10 list, weighted by what's most common for their context.

### The Universal Top 10

1. **"It's too expensive / out of our budget"**
2. **"We're not ready right now / bad timing"**
3. **"We already have a solution / we use [competitor]"**
4. **"I need to think about it"**
5. **"I need to run it by [boss/team/board]"**
6. **"We don't see the ROI / can't justify it"**
7. **"Send me some information and I'll get back to you"**
8. **"We tried something like this before and it didn't work"**
9. **"We're too busy to implement something new right now"**
10. **"We don't really have that problem"**

Adjust for context:
- **SaaS/tech:** Add "We'd need to integrate with X" and "Security/compliance concerns"
- **Agency/services:** Add "We have that in-house" and "We had a bad experience with an agency"
- **High-ticket/enterprise:** Add "We need to go through procurement" and "We'd need a pilot first"
- **SMB/founder sales:** Add "I'm the only one who can decide but I'm not sure" and "What if we just try it ourselves"

List the 10 objections you'll cover before writing any scripts.

```
WHAT IT USUALLY MEANS:

[1 sentence on the instinctive/common response that kills deals — and why]

─────────────────────────────────
SCRIPT A — [Label: what this response is trying to do]

─────────────────────────────────
```

## Step 4: The 10 Objection Frameworks

Use these as the foundation for each script. Customize the language to the user's product, buyer, and price point.

---

### OBJECTION 1: "It's too expensive / out of budget"

**What it usually means:** The value hasn't been made concrete enough to justify the number. Rarely a true budget ceiling — usually an ROI certainty gap.

**Wrong response:** Immediately offer a discount or apologize for the price.

**Framework — 3 angles:**

*A — Reframe cost as cost of inaction:*
> "I hear you on the number. Can I ask — what's the current cost of [problem this solves]? Most of our customers find that what they're spending on [workaround/inefficiency] actually exceeds what we charge. Does that math work in your situation?"

*B — Disaggregate the price:*
> "Makes sense. Let me break it down differently — at [price], that's roughly [daily/weekly/per-user cost]. Put against [specific outcome], how does that feel? I'm not trying to math you into it — just want to make sure we're comparing the right numbers."

*C — Surface the real question:*
> "Fair. Is the concern more that the number is higher than you expected, or that you're not yet sure the return justifies it? Those are different problems and I want to make sure I'm answering the right one."

---

### OBJECTION 2: "We're not ready / bad timing"

**What it usually means:** Either genuinely poor timing (budget cycle, internal chaos), OR they haven't made it a priority and "timing" is the polite exit, OR they lack internal buy-in to move.

**Wrong response:** "When would be a better time to check in?" — which hands them an easy way out with no commitment.

**Framework — 3 angles:**

*A — Qualify the timing:*
> "Totally understand. When you say timing — is it more about where you are in the budget cycle, or more that this isn't top of the priority list right now? I want to know which one I'm dealing with."

*B — Anchor to a cost of delay:*
> "That makes sense. The one thing I'd flag is [specific consequence of waiting — e.g., 'Q3 is when this typically becomes a bigger problem']. I'm not trying to manufacture urgency — just want to make sure timing doesn't end up costing more than it saves."

*C — Secure the micro-commitment:*
> "Completely fair. Would it make sense to put something on the calendar for [specific future date] — even just 15 minutes — so if things shift, we're not starting from zero? No obligation, just a placeholder."

---

### OBJECTION 3: "We already use [competitor] / happy with our current solution"

**What it usually means:** Status quo bias. Switching cost fear. Possibly genuine satisfaction — but rarely 100%. There's almost always something they wish worked better.

**Wrong response:** Badmouthing the competitor. Immediately pitching your differentiators unprompted.

**Framework — 3 angles:**

*A — Find the gap:*
> "[Competitor] is a solid tool. A lot of our customers came from there, actually. Can I ask — is there anything about it that you wish worked differently, even if it's minor? I'm not fishing for ammunition, I just find that's usually where the real conversation starts."

*B — Acknowledge and plant a seed:*
> "Makes sense — if it's working, it's working. The one question I'd leave you with: [specific capability gap or use case competitor doesn't handle well]. If that ever becomes a bigger pain point, that's exactly what we built for. Worth keeping in your back pocket?"

*C — Challenge the assumption directly:*
> "Totally fair. Can I ask one honest question — are you happy with it, or has it just become part of the furniture? There's a difference, and a lot of our customers describe their old tool the second way before they switched."

---

### OBJECTION 4: "I need to think about it"

**What it usually means:** Something is unresolved — price, risk, internal approval, a specific concern they haven't named. "Think about it" is almost never literally true; it's a holding pattern.

**Wrong response:** "Of course, take all the time you need!" — removes all urgency and hands them a clean exit.

**Framework — 3 angles:**

*A — Name the concern:*
> "Of course. Before you do — is there something specific that's giving you pause? Usually when someone needs to think about it, there's one thing that isn't quite resolved. If I know what it is, I might be able to help."

*B — Give them permission to say no:*
> "Totally. And look — if after thinking about it the answer is no, that's completely fine. I'd rather know than have this drag on. What would need to be true for it to be a yes?"

*C — Create gentle urgency:*
> "That makes sense. The one thing worth knowing: [relevant constraint — availability, pricing window, implementation timeline]. I don't want to pressure you, but I'd feel bad if that wasn't on your radar while you're deciding."

---

### OBJECTION 5: "I need to run it by my [boss/team/board]"

**What it usually means:** They're either not the decision-maker, or they are but want political cover, or they're interested but haven't secured internal buy-in.

**Wrong response:** "Of course — let me know what they say!" — removes you from the process entirely.

**Framework — 3 angles:**

*A — Get in the room:*
> "Makes total sense. Would it be helpful if I joined that conversation — even just for 15 minutes? A lot of the questions that come up are easier to answer live than over email. Is that something you could set up?"

*B — Arm your champion:*
> "Completely understand. What does your [boss/team] typically care most about in a decision like this — ROI, implementation risk, something else? I want to make sure you have the right ammo going in."

*C — Surface the stakeholder:*
> "Of course. Who's the right person to loop in on their end? Sometimes it's faster if I reach out directly so you're not playing telephone — or happy to work through you, whichever is easier."

---

### OBJECTION 6: "We don't see the ROI / can't justify it"

**What it usually means:** The value proposition hasn't been made concrete in their terms. They need numbers, not features.

**Wrong response:** Listing more features or benefits in response.

**Framework — 3 angles:**

*A — Build the ROI together:*
> "Fair — let's build it together. What would need to be true for this to pay for itself in [timeframe]? If we can get to a number that makes sense, we're having a different conversation. If we can't, I'd rather know now too."

*B — Flip to cost of inaction:*
> "Understood. Can I ask — what does [the problem] cost you today, roughly? Even a ballpark. Most people find when they add it up — time, [workaround costs], [missed revenue] — the number surprises them. I'm not trying to manipulate the math; I just want to make sure we're comparing the right things."

*C — Use a proof point:*
> "[Similar customer type] was in the same place. They were skeptical about ROI until [specific outcome — e.g., 'they ran a 60-day pilot and recovered the cost in the first month']. I can't promise identical results, but I can show you how they thought about it. Worth 20 minutes?"

---

### OBJECTION 7: "Send me some information / I'll take a look"

**What it usually means:** Polite deflection 70% of the time. Low-urgency curiosity 20%. Genuine research preference 10%.

**Wrong response:** Sending a brochure or deck with no follow-up mechanism.

**Framework — 3 angles:**

*A — Qualify before sending:*
> "Happy to. Want to make sure what I send is actually relevant — quick question: is the main thing you're trying to solve [inferred problem A] or more [problem B]? Helps me send the right thing instead of a wall of text."

*B — Trade for a conversation:*
> "Of course. Honestly though — 15 minutes on a call would tell you more than anything I could send over, and you'd know in the first 5 minutes whether it's worth your time. Worth a quick chat instead?"

*C — Send strategically, with a hook:*
> "Will do. I'll keep it short — I'll send one page with the one thing most relevant to [their situation], and I'll include the question I'd want you to be thinking about while you read it. Does [email address] work?"

---

### OBJECTION 8: "We tried something like this before and it didn't work"

**What it usually means:** Real pain from a past failure. Trust is low. They need to feel understood before they'll engage.

**Wrong response:** "We're different, here's why..." — sounds like every vendor who burned them before.

**Framework — 3 angles:**

*A — Understand the failure first:*
> "That's important context — thank you for saying that. Can I ask what happened? I'm not asking so I can tell you why we're different; I genuinely want to understand what went wrong. That usually tells me whether we'd have the same problem or a different one."

*B — Validate and differentiate carefully:*
> "Totally fair — a bad experience with one solution makes every other pitch sound the same. The thing I'd ask is: when it didn't work, was it more [root cause A — e.g., adoption, implementation, the wrong use case] or [root cause B]? That changes whether what we do is even relevant."

*C — Name the dynamic:*
> "I hear that. And I know this sounds like every other vendor saying 'but we're different.' I'm not going to do that. What I'd rather do is show you the one specific thing we do differently on [the exact failure point they described] and let you decide if it's material. Worth 10 minutes?"

---

### OBJECTION 9: "We're too busy to implement something new"

**What it usually means:** Implementation fear. They can see the value but can't envision finding time. Often tied to past bad experiences with onboarding.

**Wrong response:** Minimizing the work ("it's super easy to set up!") — sounds dismissive and unbelievable.

**Framework — 3 angles:**

*A — Address implementation directly:*
> "That's a real concern and I don't want to wave it away. What does implementation actually look like for us: [concrete specifics — e.g., 'our onboarding team does the heavy lifting, your team needs about X hours in week one, and most customers are fully live in Y days']. Does that change how you're thinking about it?"

*B — Reframe the timing:*
> "Makes sense. The counterintuitive thing: the reason most of our customers finally pulled the trigger when they were busy was that [the problem] was costing them more time than the setup would. Is that the situation here, or is it a different kind of capacity crunch?"

*C — Propose a lighter start:*
> "Totally fair. What if we scoped it down — [pilot option, limited rollout, phased approach]? Get one part of the team live with minimal lift, see what the impact looks like, and expand when timing is better. Would that feel more manageable?"

---

### OBJECTION 10: "We don't really have that problem"

**What it usually means:** Either genuinely wrong prospect (move on), OR they have the problem but don't recognize it yet, OR they've normalized the pain and don't realize it's a problem.

**Wrong response:** Arguing that they do have the problem. Or immediately folding and ending the conversation.

**Framework — 3 angles:**

*A — Qualify honestly:*
> "Good to know — I'd rather find that out now than waste both our time. Can I ask one honest question? [Specific diagnostic question that either confirms the problem or rules them out cleanly.] If the answer is no, I'll take you off my list. If it's yes, there might be something worth 10 minutes."

*B — Normalize what they're missing:*
> "That's actually what most of our customers said before they looked closely. [Specific thing most people don't realize they're losing — time, revenue, quality, something measurable.] Not saying that's you — but it's worth asking: how do you currently track/manage [the thing]?"

*C — Accept it and plant a seed:*
> "Fair enough — I'll take your word for it. The one scenario where that tends to change is [specific trigger event]. If that ever comes up, I'd love to reconnect. Is it okay if I check back in [timeframe]?"

## Step 5: The Objection Response Formula

Build this into every script so sellers understand the underlying pattern:

```
1. ACKNOWLEDGE — Validate without conceding
   "That makes sense." / "Fair point." / "I hear you."
   NOT: "Great question!" / "I completely understand!" (sycophantic)
   NOT: Silence, or immediately launching into a rebuttal

2. REFRAME — Change the lens, don't fight it
   Shift from: cost → value / timing → cost of delay / competitor → gap
   Use a question to reframe, not a statement ("Can I ask...")

3. ASK — End with a question, not a close
   The question keeps the conversation alive
   One question only — multiple questions get ignored
   The question should be one they can't answer with a yes/no

4. STOP — Don't keep talking after the question
   Silence after the question is the hardest skill. Do it.
```

---

## Step 6: Practice Mode (Optional)

If the user wants to practice rather than just read scripts, offer this:

> "Want to run through these? I'll play the prospect and hit you with objections. You respond, and I'll tell you what landed, what to tighten, and what response from the playbook fits best. Just say 'let's practice' and we'll start."

In practice mode:
- Lead with the objection verbatim, as a prospect
- After the user responds, give 2–3 sentences of coaching: what worked, what to cut, which script their response most resembles
- Don't over-coach — one or two adjustments per round
- Escalate difficulty: first round is the objection alone; second round is the objection + a follow-up resistance ("yeah but...")

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

QUICK REFERENCE CARD
[One-line reminder per objection — the single most important thing to remember]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

End with the **Quick Reference Card** — a 10-line cheat sheet the seller can keep open during calls:

```
1. Too expensive → Separate "too much" from "not worth it" — ask which one
2. Bad timing → Qualify: budget cycle or priority? Different answers.
3. Have a solution → Find the gap. "Is there anything you wish worked differently?"
4. Need to think → "What would need to be true for this to be a yes?"
5. Need approval → Get in the room or arm your champion. Don't wait outside.
6. No ROI → Build it together. "What would need to be true to pay for itself?"
7. Send info → Qualify first. "What are you mainly trying to solve?"
8. Tried before → Understand the failure before defending yourself.
9. Too busy → Make implementation concrete. Ambiguity is the real fear.
10. Don't have the problem → One diagnostic question. Rule in or rule out cleanly.
```
