# Newsletter Frameworks

Use this reference when choosing structure, improving subject lines, matching voice, or quality-checking a business newsletter.

## Newsletter structures

### 1. Problem insight lesson

Use when the user wants to teach something useful.

Structure:

1. Open with a painful or familiar problem.
2. Explain why the usual approach fails.
3. Share the better mental model or process.
4. Give 2 to 4 practical takeaways.
5. End with a CTA or question.

### 2. Founder note

Use when the sender wants to sound personal, reflective, or behind-the-scenes.

Structure:

1. Start with a real moment, observation, or tension.
2. Explain why it matters now.
3. Share the lesson or decision.
4. Connect it to the reader's work or life.
5. End with a low-pressure CTA.

### 3. Case study teardown

Use when the newsletter is based on a client, campaign, project, product, or result.

Structure:

1. State the starting problem.
2. Show the constraint or stakes.
3. Explain what changed.
4. Share the outcome.
5. Extract the repeatable lesson.
6. Invite the reader to apply or discuss it.

### 4. Curated roundup

Use when the user has multiple links, ideas, trends, or resources.

Structure:

1. Open with the theme connecting the items.
2. Present 3 to 7 curated items.
3. Add a short point of view to each item.
4. Close with the most important takeaway.

### 5. Opinion or contrarian take

Use when the user has a strong perspective.

Structure:

1. State the conventional belief.
2. Challenge it clearly.
3. Explain what people miss.
4. Support with examples or reasoning.
5. Offer a better approach.
6. End with a question or CTA.

### 6. Product or offer email

Use when the newsletter needs to drive action.

Structure:

1. Start with the problem or desired outcome.
2. Show why now matters.
3. Introduce the offer naturally.
4. Explain who it is for and what it helps with.
5. Add proof, specificity, or risk reversal.
6. End with a clear CTA.

## Subject line patterns

Use subject lines that combine clarity with curiosity.

Good patterns:

- The mistake costing [audience] [business outcome]
- A better way to [desired outcome]
- Why [common belief] is backwards
- What we learned from [specific experience]
- The [number]-part system behind [result]
- If you're trying to [goal], read this first
- The quiet reason [problem keeps happening]
- How to [outcome] without [painful tradeoff]
- I changed my mind about [topic]
- A simple fix for [pain point]

Avoid:

- You won't believe this
- This changes everything
- Last chance unless there is a real deadline
- ALL CAPS
- overpromising results
- vague lines that could apply to any topic

## Preview text patterns

Preview text should add context, not repeat the subject line.

Patterns:

- Here's the part most teams miss.
- A practical way to think about it before you invest more time or budget.
- This is the difference between sounding busy and creating real momentum.
- A short breakdown you can apply this week.
- The lesson is simple, but most teams skip it.

## CTA patterns

### Soft CTA

Use when the newsletter is primarily educational.

Examples:

- Reply and tell me where you're seeing this in your work.
- Save this for the next time you plan [task].
- Share this with someone working through the same problem.

### Direct CTA

Use when the newsletter is tied to a business offer.

Examples:

- Book a call if you want help applying this to your business.
- Download the guide here.
- Join the session here.
- Start with the checklist here.

### Conversation CTA

Use when the sender wants replies.

Examples:

- What would you add to this list?
- Have you seen this happen in your market?
- Reply with one bottleneck you're trying to solve.

## Voice diagnostics

When matching voice, look for:

- sentence length: short, medium, varied, long
- pacing: punchy, reflective, explanatory, narrative
- vocabulary: plainspoken, expert, casual, premium, technical
- warmth: low, medium, high
- confidence: tentative, balanced, bold
- humor: none, light, dry, playful
- structure: story-first, bullets-first, thesis-first, lesson-first
- CTA style: soft, direct, conversational, sales-led

## Final quality check

Before finalizing, confirm:

- The first line creates a reason to continue.
- The newsletter has one main idea.
- Each section advances that idea.
- The draft sounds like a human with a point of view.
- The value is specific enough for the audience.
- The subject lines are not misleading.
- The CTA matches the relationship with the reader.
- The draft can be sent as-is after light personalization.
