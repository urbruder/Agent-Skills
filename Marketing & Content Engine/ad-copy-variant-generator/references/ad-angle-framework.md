# Ad Angle Framework

Use this reference when creating, expanding, or evaluating ad variants.

## Core angle types

### Pain-point angle
Use when the buyer is problem-aware.
Pattern: "Tired of [pain]? [Offer] helps [audience] [better outcome]."

### Outcome angle
Use when the buyer wants a clear business result.
Pattern: "Get [desired outcome] without [common tradeoff]."

### Differentiation angle
Use when the market is crowded.
Pattern: "Most [category] tools do [old way]. [Offer] does [new way]."

### Proof angle
Use when credibility matters.
Pattern: "Built for [audience/use case], backed by [proof the user provided]."

### Speed or simplicity angle
Use when the current solution feels slow or complex.
Pattern: "Go from [current state] to [better state] in [simpler path]."

### Risk-reversal angle
Use when the buyer hesitates.
Pattern: "Try [offer] before you commit to [larger change]."

### Cost-of-inaction angle
Use when urgency must be rational, not manufactured.
Pattern: "Every month you keep [status quo], you lose [credible cost/risk]."

### Objection-handling angle
Use when prospects believe the solution is too expensive, complex, risky, or time-consuming.
Pattern: "You do not need [objection] to get [outcome]."

### Aspirational or identity angle
Use when the buyer wants to feel like a better operator, leader, founder, marketer, or team.
Pattern: "For [audience] who are done with [old way]."

### Direct offer angle
Use when the audience is warm or high-intent.
Pattern: "[Offer]: [specific value]. [CTA]."

## Platform notes

### LinkedIn
Use business outcomes, credible specificity, and professional restraint. Avoid exaggerated emotional hooks.

### Meta
Use sharper first lines, conversational copy, and benefit clarity. Keep copy easy to understand quickly.

### Google Search
Use direct keyword alignment. Match intent. Headlines should emphasize category, outcome, differentiator, and CTA.

### YouTube
Start with a strong opening line. The first sentence should make the right viewer keep watching.

### Retargeting
Assume the viewer already knows the category or offer. Focus on proof, objections, urgency, offer, or next step.

## Quality checklist

A strong ad variant:

- has one dominant idea
- is meaningfully different from the other variants
- makes the buyer's problem or desired outcome obvious
- connects the offer to a credible mechanism or proof point
- includes a clear next action
- avoids claims that require evidence unless evidence was provided

## Claim safety

Do not invent:

- percentages
- time savings
- revenue improvements
- customer logos
- awards
- certifications
- case study results
- guarantees

When proof is missing, use cautious phrasing:

- "designed to help"
- "built to reduce"
- "aims to make"
- "can help teams"
- "for teams looking to"

## Testing guidance

Do not test only tiny wording changes in the first round. Test major angle differences first. Once a winning angle appears, create micro-variants of that winner.
